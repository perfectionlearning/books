Conduction, Convection, and Radiation are three ways that thermal energy is transferred. You will use your powers of observation and perform a few experiments to determine how thermal energy moves through various states of matter. You can demonstrate each form of this energy transfer through solids, gases and liquids (except for convection, which does not occur within solids).

Safety

Thermal Energy Safety:

Do not touch heated surfaces without proper protection, such as insulated gloves.

Let hot liquids, solids, or gases cool down before transferring them or cleaning them up.

Do not expose any part of your body to steam.

General Lab Safety: Observe all procedures detailed by your instructor.

Keep a clean and orderly work space.

If you break something or spill something, notify your instructor immediately.

If someone needs first aid, notify your instructor immediately.

When you have completed an experiment, always dispose of, recycle, or store equipment and supplies in their proper location.

Exercise 1: Observing the Transfer of Thermal Energy

In Exercise 1, we want you to consider the transfer of thermal energy using a variety of materials in your classroom.

Question

Which methods of thermal energy transfer can be observed in solids, liquids, and gases?

Hypothesis


A hypothesis is a brief statement that describes what you expect your experiment to show. For example, in this question you have to decide whether thermal energy is transferred by conduction, convection, or radiation to solids, liquids, and/or gases.

State your hypothesis for the question above.

Design of the Experiment

In this experiment you will observe a variety of materials to determine if thermal energy has been transferred.

Identify the Variables

Thermal energy, temperature, various objects and/or substances

Steps in the Experiment

Create a Data Collection Chart

  1. Column 1: Name of object or substance observed
  2. Column 2: Observation location (for example, a sunny window)
  3. Column 3: Was thermal energy transferred? Yes/No
  4. Columns 4-6: Labeled Convection, Conduction, and Radiation, respectively


Conduct Your Observations

  1. As you follow the steps below, note in your Data Collection chart whether or not thermal energy was transferred and the method by which you think the energy was transferred.
  2. Place the following in the sunlight:
    1. Wooden craft stick
    2. Glass rod
    3. Black construction paper
    4. Piece of aluminum foil
    5. Small balloon filled with air (Don’t forget to anchor this in position; you might tie a string onto the knotted end of the balloon and either use a weight at the other end of the string or tie it in place.)
    6. Petri dish with water
    7. Petri dish with water placed on top of black construction paper
    8. Petri dish with water placed on top of a piece of aluminum foil
    9. Using the thermometer, measure the temperature of the air closest to the sunlit window and compare it to the air temperature in the rest of the classroom.
  1. Place the same items from step 6 away from the sunlit window and in front of a fan.
  2. Place the following in a dishpan of very warm water:
    1. Wooden craft stick
    2. Glass rod
    3. Metal spoon
    4. Plastic spoon
    5. Air-filled balloon
    6. Beaker with ice
    7. Beaker with water
    8. Using the thermometer, measure the temperature of the air closest to the container of very warm water and compare it to the air temperature in the rest of the classroom.
  1. Put on safety goggles. Then place the following on a hot plate and turn on the heat to medium. As another option, use a ring stand, ring clamps, clamps, and 90-degree rod clamps to hold a 4-inch ring and a ring stand screen over a Bunsen burner. Place the beakers on the ring stand screen for several minutes.
    1. Beaker of tap water
    2. Beaker of ice
    3. Using a thermometer, measure the temperature of the contents before and after the thermal energy is added to the beaker of tap water, and to the beaker of ice.
    4. When you have completed your observations, turn off the heat and allow the containers to cool. Remove using a thermal glove and place on a heat resistant surface or hot pad until completely cool.

  1. Place the following in a microwave oven in a microwave safe container for 20 to 30 seconds:
    1. water
    2. ice
    3. Using a thermometer, measure the temperature of the contents before and after the thermal energy is added to the beaker of tap water, and to the beaker of ice.
    4. When you have completed your observations, remove the items using a thermal glove and place on a heat resistant surface or hot pad until completely cool.

  1. Clean up all items used and store them according to your instructor’s directions.


Data Collection

Record all of your observations in your Data Collection chart as you perform each step of the experiment.

Analyze Your Results

Compare and Contrast

  1. Did the material the object/substance was made of make a difference in whether thermal energy was transferred? How did you know that thermal energy was transferred? Explain why you think this is the case.
  2. What was the most frequent method of heat transfer you encountered—conduction, convection, or radiation? Explain why you think this was the case.

Evaluate Your Data
Is Your Data Reasonable?
  1. Are you getting the same general results as other lab groups?

Determine Sources of Uncertainty

  1. In every experiment there are potential sources of error and/or instances of a lack of accuracy and precision. Explain what sources of error and lack of accuracy and precision might be in this experiment.




Form Your Conclusion


Think Critically



  1. If your experiments proved your hypotheses, your conclusion is merely a restatement of your hypotheses.
  2. If your experiments did not prove your hypotheses, you may have to design additional experiments and form new hypotheses based on your new experimental data. If this is the case, ask your instructor if there is time to redesign and re-run one or more of your experiments.
  3. What scientific laws or theories have you studied that support your experiences in these experiments?


State Your Conclusion

  1. After considering the information in the Think Critically section and reviewing the experiences, observations, data, and calculations of this experiment, state your Conclusion. Be sure to compare your results to the law and theories you have studied and to explain your reasoning.
