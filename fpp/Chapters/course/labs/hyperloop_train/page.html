The hyperloop train will get you quickly from Los Angeles to San Francisco – if the passengers don’t experience too much discomfort as the centripetal acceleration pushes them toward the side of the train. And if the train doesn’t go off the tracks.

This lab covers the following topics:
  • Centripetal acceleration
  • The effect of turn radius on centripetal acceleration
  • The relationship between speed on a turn and centripetal acceleration
  • NOTE: a more general coverage of the concepts of linear and centripetal acceleration can be found in the Uniform Circular Motion lab.
Exercise 1: Centripetal acceleration

The track is divided into three segments. In the first segment, your train’s speed increases. Here, your train will go from rest (0 m/s) to the speed you select for it, up to a maximum of 250 m/s. (A good exercise: convert this to kmh and mph.)

When you enter the simulation, notice the gauges for the train on the control panel:

  • On the left is a speed gauge. It resembles a standard car speedometer, although the readout is in meters per second. 10 m/s equals 36 km/hr, which is about 22 mph.
  • The next gauge shows acceleration. It indicates the magnitude of your train’s acceleration, in m/s2.
  • To the right of that is a horizontally-shaped gauge for x velocity. It shows the component of your train’s velocity along the x axis, that is, in the horizontal direction on your screen.
  • The final, vertically-shaped gauge is for y velocity. It shows the component of the train’s velocity along the y axis, which is the vertical direction on your screen.
