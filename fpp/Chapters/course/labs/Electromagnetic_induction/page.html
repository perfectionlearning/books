A magnetic field can be used to induce, or create, an electromotive force (emf). This emf can drive an electric current. Electromagnetic induction is the basis for the generation of most of the electricity that is produced in the world today.

Electromagnetic induction can also be used to change or transform an emf (a voltage). It is used in devices called transformers that increase or decrease the voltage of an alternating current power supply.

In this lab, you will experiment with a way to induce an emf and to “generate” electricity. You will do so by spinning a wire loop in a magnetic field.

You will then experiment with mutual induction, using the change in the magnetic field produced by one circuit to induce an emf in a second circuit. This is the phenomenon that underlies the functioning of transformers. It is used, for instance, to reduce the dangerously high potential differences in “high voltage lines” to a safer level of potential difference that can be used in the home.

Exercise 1: A generator

A wire moving perpendicularly to a magnetic field “generates” an electric current. An industrial electric generator operates by the same principle, only it involves rotating a wire coil through a field. Here, the coil is just a single loop of wire.

The magnetic force acts on electrons in the wire. The amount of the force can be calculated using an equation: F = |q|vB sin θ.

In this exercise we focus on the emf generated in the lengthwise wire segments that are parallel to the loop’s axis of rotation. We show the velocity of one of these segments as a black vector. The other parts of the loop make no net contribution to the emf. The rotating loop is connected by the two “leads” you see in the illustration to a complete circuit, which is not shown.

As the loop rotates through the magnetic field, the angle between the segment’s velocity and the field changes: The angle θ in the formula changes. At some points, the segment is moving perpendicularly to the field. At other points, it moves parallel to the field. The strength and orientation of the emf changes as the loop rotates. The current changes in strength and orientation along with the emf.

All this is simpler to see in the simulation, since it allows you to see the emf, and you can change the viewing angle, which proves highly useful.

In the simulation, you control a single parameter, the angular velocity ω of the loop. An output gauge displays the angle between the velocity of a segment of the wire and the magnetic field.

Experiment with the generator in this simulation. When you open the simulation, the single loop is motionless in a magnetic field. You start it rotating by clicking the up button next to the angular velocity (ω) label and clicking on GO. Once you do this, a current will flow through the circuit and you can use the oscilloscope to view and measure the induced emf.

There is a readout-only display of the angle θ. The red current arrows in the simulation represent the direction and relative magnitude of the conventional current.

Exercise 2: Using mutual induction to transform voltage

What if you want to induce a voltage (sometimes called emf, short for “electromotive force”) in a secondary circuit that has a different value from the voltage in a primary circuit? Can you think of a way to do this? You can do this by causing the amount of magnetic field passing through the circuit (called the magnetic flux) to differ between the two.

How can you have a different magnetic flux through the two circuits when the same magnetic field is passing through both? One answer is: change the effective surface area of one circuit to make it different from the surface area of the other. Increasing the number of loops increases the effective surface area the magnetic field passes through, and this increases the magnetic flux.

In the simulation for this section, we have fixed the value for the amplitude of the alternating emf in the primary set of coils, called a solenoid. The current in this circuit creates an “alternating” (oscillating) magnetic field that passes unchanged through the second solenoid.

You can control the number of loops in the secondary circuit. Your task here is to observe what effect this has on induced emf. By varying the number of loops in the secondary circuit, you should be able to determine the general strategy for increasing (stepping up) a voltage or decreasing (stepping down) a voltage.

Open the simulation and you will see an experimental setup, with primary and secondary coils wrapped around a central iron core. In this simulation, you can set the number of coils in the secondary solenoid, from 10 loops to 50 in increments of 5, using the up-down buttons on the control panel.

An alternating voltage (“emf”) is applied to the primary circuit. Observe the induced emf in the secondary circuit using the oscilloscope when the number of loops in both circuits is the same.

Then change the number of loops in the secondary circuit. Do this systematically to determine the effect this has on the induced emf. Example: What happens to the induced emf if the number of secondary loops is half the number in the primary? What if it has twice as many loops?
