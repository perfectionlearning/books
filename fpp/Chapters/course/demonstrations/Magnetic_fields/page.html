Below is the list of equipment that you will need for the demonstration and the steps to follow.
Equipment
  • Iron filings
  • Bar magnet
  • Horseshoe magnet
  • White craft paper
  • Magnetic compass

Safety

Magnets can cause malfunctioning in electronic devices, such as laptops, pacemakers, or portable music players. They can also erase data stored on the “magnetic stripe” on credit cards, etc.

Small magnets should be kept away from young children, as they may be swallowed.

Steps in Demonstration 1

  1. Place a bar magnet on a table.
  2. Cover it with a piece of white craft paper. The dark filings will contrast nicely against the white paper. If you have a document camera, you may show the demonstration to a large class. Alternatively, if you have an overhead projector, use translucent paper such as wax paper or tracing paper.
  3. Sprinkle the iron filings on the paper.
  4. Slightly shake or tap the paper to help the iron filings move and settle down.
  5. Ask students to draw the pattern they see in the filings.
Steps in Demonstration 2

  1. Reverse the north/south direction of the magnet on the table.
  2. Repeat steps 2 through 5 above.
  3. Draw the pattern you see in the filings.
  4. Ask: “Can you tell what direction the magnet is facing from the pattern of the filings?”
Steps in Demonstration 3

  1. Place a pair of bar magnets with opposite poles (N and S) near each other on the table. Have the ends separated by about 2 centimeters.
  2. Repeat steps 2 through 5 above.
  3. Ask: “How does the pattern compare to that of a single magnet?”
  4. Ask: “If you see this pattern and know what one of the poles of one magnet is, can you determine what the orientation of the other magnet is?”
Steps in Demonstration 4

  1. Place two bar magnets in a line with their south poles facing each other. Leave a gap of about 2 centimeters between the ends of the magnets.
  2. Repeat steps 2 through 5 above.
  3. Ask: “If you see this pattern and know what one of the poles of one magnet is, can you determine what the orientation of the other magnet is?”
Steps in Demonstration 5

  1. Place two bar magnets in a line with north poles facing each other. Leave a gap of about 2 centimeters between the ends of the magnets.
  2. Ask: “What pattern do you predict you will see when you scatter the filings onto the white craft paper?”
  3. Repeat steps 2 through 5 above.
Steps in Demonstration 6

  1. Place two bar magnets on the table next to and parallel to each other, with the north pole of one magnet next to the north pole of the other. You may have to tape the magnets together, as they will tend to repel each other.
  2. Ask: “What pattern do you predict for the filings?”
  3. Repeat steps 2 through 5 above.
Steps in Demonstration 7

  1. Use a horseshoe magnet.
  2. Ask: “What pattern can you predict for the filings?”
  3. Repeat steps 2 through 5 above.
Steps in Demonstration 8

  1. Place a magnetic compass on the table, far from any other magnets. Tap it gently so that the needle is free to reach equilibrium.
  2. Ask: “Which way does the needle point?” (If it does not point roughly toward geographic north, you may have a defective compass, or there may be stray magnetic fields in the area.)
  3. Now bring the south pole of a bar magnet near the compass and move it around.
  4. Ask students to write down their observations about the behavior of the compass needle.
  5. Ask: “Based on your previous observation, what kind of magnetic pole (N or S) is found near the Earth’s geographic north pole?”
